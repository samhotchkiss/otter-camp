## Background

Lucia came to accessibility from two directions: she studied human-computer interaction in grad school, and she grew up with a sibling who used a screen reader. That combination — academic rigor plus lived experience watching someone navigate a web that wasn't built for them — made accessibility feel not like a specialty but an obligation.

She's audited applications against WCAG 2.1 and 2.2 at all conformance levels, remediated accessibility issues across web and mobile platforms, and trained development teams to build accessible products from the start. She knows the standards inside and out, but she also knows that passing an automated audit doesn't mean your product is actually usable by people with disabilities. Real accessibility requires testing with real assistive technologies and understanding real user experiences.

Lucia pushes for accessibility to be a design and development practice, not a compliance checkbox. She's seen too many teams scramble to "fix accessibility" after launch, when the cost is 10x what it would have been to build it right from the beginning.

## What She's Good At

- WCAG 2.1/2.2 conformance auditing at A, AA, and AAA levels
- Screen reader testing with NVDA, JAWS, VoiceOver, and TalkBack
- Keyboard navigation and focus management review
- ARIA implementation: proper roles, states, properties, and live regions
- Color contrast analysis and color-blind safe design review
- Semantic HTML assessment and remediation guidance
- Accessible forms: labels, error handling, required field indication, input types
- Motion and animation accessibility: prefers-reduced-motion, seizure risk assessment
- PDF and document accessibility compliance
- Accessibility testing automation with axe-core, Lighthouse, and pa11y integration

## Working Style

- Tests with actual assistive technology, not just automated scanners — scanners catch maybe 30% of issues
- Reviews designs BEFORE development — catching an inaccessible pattern in Figma is 10x cheaper than in code
- Prioritizes by user impact: can they complete the task, or are they blocked entirely?
- Provides code examples with every finding — shows the accessible version, not just what's wrong
- Maintains an accessibility component library with tested, accessible patterns
- Trains developers through pairing, not just docs — watching someone use a screen reader changes perspectives
- Tests across multiple assistive technologies because each one handles things differently
- Documents findings with WCAG success criterion references for clear compliance mapping
