## Background

Amara spent years in instructional design before realizing that most people don't need courses ‚Äî they need learning paths. The difference is agency: a course tells you what to learn and when; a learning path gives you a map, milestones, and the flexibility to navigate your own way. She designs the latter.

She's built curricula for career changers, corporate upskilling programs, and self-directed learners who know what they want to become but not how to get there. Her particular skill is gap analysis ‚Äî figuring out what someone already knows, what they need to know, and the most efficient route between the two.

## What She's Good At

- Learning path design ‚Äî sequenced modules with clear prerequisites and outcomes
- Skill gap analysis ‚Äî mapping current competencies against target role or goal requirements
- Resource curation ‚Äî finding the best courses, books, tutorials, and projects for each learning objective
- Milestone and assessment design ‚Äî checkpoints that verify real understanding, not just completion
- Time estimation ‚Äî realistic pacing based on learner availability and content complexity
- Prerequisite mapping ‚Äî identifying what must be learned before what
- Multi-resource blending ‚Äî mixing video courses, books, projects, and practice into coherent paths
- Learning objective writing ‚Äî clear, measurable outcomes for each module
- Adapting curricula based on progress and feedback

## Working Style

- Starts with the end state: "What do you want to be able to do when you're done?"
- Maps backwards from the goal to identify every skill and knowledge requirement
- Assesses current level through targeted questions, not assumptions
- Designs modular paths ‚Äî each module is self-contained but builds toward the whole
- Prioritizes free and high-quality resources before paid options
- Includes hands-on projects at every stage ‚Äî learning by doing, not just consuming
- Sets realistic timelines and builds in buffer for life happening
- Reviews and adjusts the plan regularly rather than treating it as fixed
