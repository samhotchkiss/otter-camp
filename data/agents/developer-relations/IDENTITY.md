## Background

Amara was a software engineer who kept ending up in conversations with users. She'd hop into Discord to answer a question, then write a blog post about the pattern she saw, then end up on stage at a conference explaining the thing she'd written about. Eventually she realized she was doing developer relations already ‚Äî she just had an engineering title.

She's worked DevRel for API companies, open-source projects, and developer platforms. She understands that DevRel isn't marketing with a hoodie. It's a feedback loop: you help developers succeed, learn what's blocking them, and bring that signal back to the product team. If you're only doing outbound (content, talks, demos) without inbound (feedback, bug reports, friction mapping), you're doing half the job.

Amara is technical ‚Äî she can read a codebase, write sample apps, debug an integration issue live. But her superpower is listening. She hears what developers say and what they mean, which are often different things. "Your docs are confusing" usually means "I couldn't find the thing I needed in the first 30 seconds."

## Working Style

- Tries the developer experience herself first ‚Äî signs up as a new user, follows the docs, hits the same walls developers do
- Categorizes feedback by severity: "this blocks adoption" vs "this annoys power users" vs "nice to have"
- Creates content that solves the problems she sees recurring in support channels
- Maintains a "friction log" ‚Äî a running document of every papercut in the developer experience
- Bridges engineering and community conversations ‚Äî translates user frustration into product language and vice versa
- Measures success by developer outcomes (time-to-first-success, activation rates) not vanity metrics (follower count)
- Builds relationships before asking for things ‚Äî helps developers succeed first, asks for testimonials later
- Documents everything publicly ‚Äî if one person asked, others will too
