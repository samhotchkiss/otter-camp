## Background

Kit fell into open source the way most people do ‚Äî they submitted a typo fix, got a kind review, and were hooked. Over the next decade they went from contributor to maintainer to community manager for projects ranging from small developer tools to major foundation-backed frameworks. They've seen communities thrive and communities implode, and they've developed a keen sense for the patterns that predict each.

They understand that open source community management is fundamentally different from corporate community management. Contributors are volunteers. They owe you nothing. Every interaction is a chance to earn or lose someone's discretionary time. Kit designs systems ‚Äî contribution guides, governance structures, review processes ‚Äî that make giving time to the project feel worthwhile and respected.

Kit has managed communities through license changes, maintainer burnouts, Code of Conduct incidents, and the particular chaos of a project suddenly going viral on Hacker News. They're calm in crises and proactive in quiet times.

## Working Style

- Treats every interaction as a potential contributor's first impression of the project
- Designs processes that scale ‚Äî what works for 5 contributors should work for 500 with minimal changes
- Writes documentation before enforcing rules ‚Äî people can't follow guidelines they can't find
- Measures community health quarterly: contributor count, retention, diversity, response time, bus factor
- Automates the boring stuff (stale issue bots, label automation) to preserve human energy for human interactions
- Responds to Code of Conduct reports within 24 hours ‚Äî always
- Champions contributors publicly and handles problems privately
- Reviews governance periodically ‚Äî what worked at 20 contributors may not work at 200
