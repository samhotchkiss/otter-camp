Building with AI Agents: A Practical Guide

How to delegate effectively and stay in control when your team includes autonomous AI systems

By Sam Hotchkiss ‚Ä¢ February 2026

The first time I delegated a task to an AI agent and walked away, I felt like a parent watching their kid ride a bike without training wheels. Equal parts pride and terror. What if it crashes? What if it does something I didn't anticipate?

Six months later, I run a team of 13 AI agents. They handle engineering, content, social media, email triage, and more. Some work autonomously for hours. Others check in frequently. The key insight I've learned: the hard part isn't getting AI to do work ‚Äî it's designing systems where you stay informed without micromanaging.1

The Trust Calibration Problem

When you first start working with AI agents, you face what I call the "trust calibration problem." You don't know what to trust them with. You don't know when they'll fail. And they don't know when to ask for help.

This creates two failure modes:

Under-delegation: You check every output, approve every action, and essentially use the AI as an autocomplete. You're doing the work yourself with extra steps.

Over-delegation: You hand off a complex task, disappear for hours, and come back to find the agent went down a rabbit hole, burned through your API budget, or produced something completely off-base.

The solution isn't to find the "right" level of trust and stay there. It's to build feedback loops that let trust calibrate itself over time.

Designing for Ambient Awareness

The goal of a good human-AI workflow isn't to eliminate human involvement ‚Äî it's to make human involvement effortless and well-timed.2 You want to know what's happening without having to ask. You want to intervene when it matters without watching constantly.

I call this "ambient awareness," and it requires three things:

  1. Status at a glance. Your dashboard should tell you the state of all work in 3 seconds. Not a wall of text ‚Äî visual indicators.
  2. Escalation by exception. Agents should work quietly until something needs you. Then it should be obvious and interruptive.
  3. Audit trails you'll actually read. Every decision logged, but presented as a skimmable timeline, not a log file.

The "Context Block" Pattern

Here's a specific pattern that's worked well for us: every task assigned to an agent includes a "context block" ‚Äî a self-contained summary of everything needed to complete the work.

## Context Block

**Files:** src/auth/login.ts, src/auth/session.ts
**Decisions made:** JWT over session cookies, 24h expiry
**Acceptance criteria:**
- [ ] Login endpoint returns valid JWT
- [ ] Invalid credentials return 401
- [ ] Rate limiting prevents brute force
**Related knowledge:** See SECURITY.md for auth patterns

The rule is simple: an agent should be able to pick up any task with only the context block and the codebase. No conversation history. No "remember when we discussed..." If it's not in the context block, it doesn't exist.

This pattern has a secondary benefit: it forces you to think clearly about requirements before delegating. If you can't write a context block, you don't understand the task well enough to delegate it.

When to Interrupt

Not all work requires the same level of human involvement. I categorize agent tasks into three buckets:

Green: Safe to complete autonomously. Internal refactoring, test writing, research synthesis.
Yellow: Complete but hold for review. Anything customer-facing, any external communication.
Red: Stop and ask. Spending money, deleting data, anything irreversible.

The colors aren't about risk exactly ‚Äî they're about reversibility. Can I undo this if the agent got it wrong? If yes, let it run. If no, make it ask first.

The Crankfeed vs. The Inbox

Our system splits activity into two views: the Crankfeed and the Inbox.

The Crankfeed is ambient. It's a timeline of everything happening ‚Äî commits, status changes, comments. You skim it. You don't act on it. It's there for situational awareness, not task management.

The Inbox is actionable. Everything in it requires a decision. Approve, reject, answer a question, unblock something. When your inbox is empty, you're done. You don't need to check anything else.

The key insight: don't mix awareness and action. Mixing them creates anxiety ‚Äî you're never sure if you're "done."3 Separating them creates calm ‚Äî the inbox is your todo list, the feed is just scenery.

Getting Started

If you're just starting with AI agents, here's my advice: start with a single, well-defined task. Something that takes you 30 minutes and has clear success criteria. Watch the agent work. See where it struggles. Adjust your instructions.

Then gradually expand scope. Add a second task. Then a third. Build the feedback loops as you go. Trust will calibrate itself if you let it.

The future isn't AI replacing human judgment ‚Äî it's AI amplifying it. Your job isn't to do the work. It's to design systems where work gets done well, and you stay informed enough to steer.

That's the practical reality of building with AI agents. It's less "magic automation" and more "thoughtful delegation." But once you get the patterns right, it's genuinely transformative.

Your team just got a lot bigger.
